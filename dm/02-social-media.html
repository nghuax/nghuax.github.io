Facebook · An Thai · RACE

Social Media Evaluation.

01

The growth of social media.

Social media has become one of the defining commercial infrastructures of the last two decades. By the start of 2025, DataReportal (2025a) reports that global social-media identities have continued to climb beyond five billion, making social platforms the single largest attention surface available to marketers. For brands, this scale is significant because it collapses the traditional distance between broadcast media and direct customer contact into a single environment (Chaffey & Ellis-Chadwick, 2022).

The commercial opportunity of social media is not limited to reach. Social platforms also support traffic generation, customer service, community building and — increasingly — direct transactions (Solomon & Tuten, 2018). For a brand such as An Thai, whose credibility has been built over nearly three decades, social media offers the surface on which that credibility can be restated in a more human, contemporary register.

02

The growth of Facebook in Vietnam.

Within Vietnam specifically, Facebook is the leading social platform by scale and routine use. DataReportal (2025a) reports 76.2 million Facebook users in Vietnam at the start of 2025, alongside 55.9 million Messenger users (DataReportal, 2025b). Against a total internet population of 79.8 million, these figures mean that the overlap between Facebook and the wider Vietnamese online population is near-total.

For marketers, Facebook in Vietnam is therefore not merely a social network but a commercial infrastructure. It functions simultaneously as a broadcast channel, a customer-service surface (through Messenger) and a community environment. This dual identity — broadcast and service — is what makes Facebook the single most commercially relevant social platform for An Thai to evaluate.

03

Facebook users by age and gender.

Reported demographic data from DataReportal (2025a) indicates that Vietnamese Facebook usage is distributed broadly across working-age cohorts, with the 25–34 segment the largest single audience group and near-equal representation across male and female users. For a coffee brand with both mass-market (3-in-1 mix) and premium (freeze-dried, Vua Chồn) product lines, this demographic profile is favourable: the same channel reaches routine daily-use buyers and gifting-oriented consumers simultaneously.

The strategic implication for An Thai is that Facebook does not require audience fragmentation. A single, well-run page can serve multiple product lines and purchase occasions — provided that the content mix is calibrated to each cohort's context (office drinking, home brewing, gifting, Tết and seasonal occasions).

04

The RACE framework.

Dave Chaffey's RACE framework treats digital marketing as a connected funnel of four stages — Reach, Act, Convert and Engage — each of which corresponds to a distinct marketing objective (Chaffey, 2023; Chaffey & Smith, 2023). The framework is widely used to evaluate whether a given channel contributes meaningfully across the full customer journey, rather than only at the top of the funnel.

  • Reach. Building awareness of the brand through paid, owned and earned media so that potential customers encounter the brand in the first place.
  • Act. Encouraging an interaction or enquiry — a comment, a click, a message, a product view — that moves attention toward consideration.
  • Convert. Turning consideration into a commercial outcome: a purchase, a sample request, a distributor enquiry, a sign-up.
  • Engage. Building repeat interaction and loyalty through sustained content, service and relationship management.

The four RACE stages are not independent. A platform that is strong at Reach but weak at Convert is leaking value further down the funnel; a platform that excels at Engage but cannot sustain Reach will plateau. The evaluation below applies each stage to An Thai's Facebook presence.

05

How An Thai uses Facebook
— analysis based on the RACE framework.

The following evaluation examines each RACE stage using An Thai's current Facebook activity and the scale of the Vietnamese Facebook audience as the frame of reference.

05.1

Reach.

Under Reach, Facebook performs strongly for An Thai. The platform gives the brand access to a large Vietnamese audience — 76.2 million users — and supports brand storytelling around coffee origin, product range, gifting and café lifestyle (DataReportal, 2025a). Because An Thai's credibility assets (heritage since 1996, thirty-plus export markets, ISO/HACCP/Halal/BRC/Kosher/SNI certifications) are already well documented on its corporate channels (An Thai Group, n.d.), Facebook provides a natural surface on which those assets can be translated into shorter, more human-facing content.

The main limitation at the Reach stage is not An Thai's content but Facebook's algorithm: organic reach fluctuates and cannot be relied on without supplementary paid promotion. This is a platform characteristic rather than a brand weakness.

05.2

Act.

Under Act, Facebook is also effective for An Thai. Users can react, click, comment and message the brand quickly, which makes the platform a useful surface for turning awareness into interest and enquiry (Chaffey & Ellis-Chadwick, 2022). Messenger's 55.9 million Vietnamese users (DataReportal, 2025b) means that “ask the brand” is a low-friction behaviour rather than a formal request channel — which matches the informal, conversational register Vietnamese consumers already use when contacting brands.

The limitation at the Act stage is that not all interaction converts. A user who reacts to a post or asks a product question through Messenger has not yet purchased; the commercial value of that interaction depends entirely on what happens next. That question is addressed under Convert.

05.3

Convert.

Under Convert, the evaluation identifies the main weakness in An Thai's current Facebook activity. Conversion from Facebook works well only when the platform is connected to a clear landing or purchase path. In An Thai's case, several consumer products on the AnTháiCafé website are listed with “Liên hệ” (“Contact”) rather than a transparent price and buy-path (AnTháiCafé, n.d.). For B2B and distributor enquiries this pattern is defensible; for the consumer journey that Facebook traffic overwhelmingly represents, it is a hard commercial stop.

The result is that even when Facebook successfully moves a user from Reach to Act, the handoff to a purchase decision is uncertain. This is not a Facebook problem — it is an architecture problem that Facebook surfaces. Tightening the link between Facebook posts and product pages with visible prices, marketplace listings or distributor links would be the single most impactful Convert-stage improvement.

05.4

Engage.

Under Engage, Facebook retains significant value for An Thai because it supports repeated exposure, comment-based dialogue, Messenger familiarity and service responsiveness — all of which are commercially valuable for a repeat-purchase category such as coffee (Brodie et al., 2011; Bowden, 2009). However, sustained engagement depends on consistent content quality and fast response times. Without an operational cadence, the Engage stage collapses back into Reach: the brand is seen but not remembered.

Concrete improvements at the Engage stage would include a documented Messenger response time, a weekly content rhythm (origin story · product spotlight · UGC repost · service answer), and a retention loop — for example, a follow-up Messenger message three weeks after a first purchase with a reorder link and a seasonal product nudge. Together, these moves turn Facebook from a one-off touchpoint into a managed relationship channel.

Overall verdict. Facebook is a strong platform choice for An Thai, but only when used as part of a wider digital system. Managers should view Facebook as the company's lead community and service channel, not as a stand-alone sales engine. The commercial task is to connect Facebook more tightly to product pages, distributor routes and message-handling processes.
