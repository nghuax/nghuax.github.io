Website Checklist.

01

Why a good website matters.

For most brands today, the website is the single most persistent digital asset — the place potential customers arrive at from social posts, search queries, advertising, and word of mouth. A well-designed website does more than display information. It should reduce search effort, increase trust, support decision-making, and make action easy (Chaffey & Ellis-Chadwick, 2022; Barker, 2020).

The consequence of poor design is straightforward: visitors abandon the site. Research on e-commerce usability shows that information convenience, page speed, clarity of product pages and the visibility of reviews are among the strongest predictors of whether a visit ends in a purchase (Salehi et al., 2012; Liu, Karahanna & Watson, 2011; Gonçalves et al., 2017). For An Thai — a brand whose trust assets are strong but whose commercial structure on the web is less developed — the website is therefore a critical surface.

02

The growth of e-commerce in Vietnam.

Vietnam has become one of the fastest-growing e-commerce markets in Southeast Asia. The Vietnam E-Commerce Association (VECOM, 2025) reports that the country's e-commerce market reached US$32 billion in 2024, with a growth rate of 27%, while online retail sales reached US$22.5 billion, growing 30% year-on-year. Globally, e-commerce retail sales are forecast to continue expanding well into the second half of the decade (Expert Panel, 2024).

This context matters because it determines what a brand's website is being measured against. In a market where online retail is growing by thirty per cent a year, a website that is “informationally adequate” is no longer commercially adequate. Visitors arriving from Facebook, Google or a distributor link expect transparent pricing, a buy-path, mobile legibility and credible reviews. The absence of any one of these is visible competitive ground given up.

03

Criteria to evaluate an e-commerce website.

Drawing on the literature, four broad criteria are used below to evaluate a consumer-facing e-commerce website (Chaffey & Ellis-Chadwick, 2022; Barker, 2020; Expert Panel, 2024). These four criteria form the checklist applied to AnTháiCafé in Section 5.

a. Functional content

The first criterion is whether the website communicates the information that visitors need in order to make a decision. This includes detailed product information, visible customer reviews, FAQ sections addressing common queries, and clear contact options (Liu, Karahanna & Watson, 2011; Engler, Winter & Schulz, 2015). Functional content is what turns a site from a brochure into a decision-support surface.

b. Ease of use

The second criterion concerns accessibility, usability and navigation. A good website makes its structure legible within seconds: menus are consistent, categories are meaningful, and users can move from homepage to product to checkout without guesswork. Gonçalves et al. (2017) show that usability problems disproportionately affect conversion; Kuo, Chang and Lai (2021) note that visual clarity supports comprehension.

c. Well-aesthetic design

The third criterion covers colour, typography and layout — the visual system that signals whether a brand is contemporary, credible and coherent (Kuo, Chang & Lai, 2021; Barker, 2020). Aesthetic design is not decoration: it is the first proxy users have for how much care a brand has taken with its product. An inconsistent or dated visual system undermines otherwise strong content.

d. On-page SEO

The fourth criterion covers technical signals that determine whether a site is discoverable and fast: page titles, meta descriptions, heading structure, image alt text and load time (Ghimire, 2022; Giomelakis & Veglis, 2015). SEO is increasingly a usability issue as much as a marketing one: slow or poorly structured pages frustrate users and are penalised by search algorithms simultaneously.

05

An example of website checklist
— AnTháiCafé.

AnTháiCafé is the consumer-facing website for An Thai, carrying product ranges such as Vua Chồn (King Weasel), origin content and contact details (AnTháiCafé, n.d.). The following audit applies the four criteria in turn.

05.a

a. Functional content.

AnTháiCafé's product pages communicate the brand's range — instant, freeze-dried, 3-in-1 and extracted liquid products — with descriptions and category navigation. The company's broader credibility assets (heritage since 1996, thirty-plus export markets, ISO/HACCP/Halal/BRC/Kosher/SNI certifications) are clearly surfaced on the corporate site (An Thai Group, n.d.). Contact options, including hotline and email, are visible and provide strong reassurance for both consumer and B2B enquiries.

The functional content weakness is concentrated in two places. First, customer reviews and ratings are not consistently surfaced on product pages, which limits social proof at the decision point (Liu, Karahanna & Watson, 2011; Cialdini, 2001). Second, several consumer products are listed with “Liên hệ” (“Contact”) in place of price and buy-path, which leaves a key functional information gap on the site.

05.b

b. Ease of use.

Navigation on AnTháiCafé is serviceable. The top menu exposes the major product categories and the main information sections (company, products, contact), and the category architecture is legible to a first-time visitor. A user arriving from Facebook or a search result can generally understand what the site is about within a few seconds — a reasonable baseline for the Ease of Use criterion.

The weaker sub-criterion is mobile usability, which cannot be scored with high confidence without controlled device-level evidence. Because Vietnam's internet traffic is overwhelmingly mobile (DataReportal, 2025a), this is the sub-score that would most benefit from a dedicated screenshot-backed re-audit before being treated as final.

05.c

c. Well-aesthetic design.

AnTháiCafé's visual system leans heavily on brand heritage — traditional coffee imagery, warm tones and product photography of the Vua Chồn range (AnTháiCafé, n.d.). This aesthetic is consistent with a brand whose positioning is craft-led and established rather than start-up contemporary. Typography is readable and the visual hierarchy is functional.

The opportunity on the aesthetic criterion is modernisation of product photography and a tighter typographic system that better showcases the brand's premium lines alongside its mass-market products. This is a refinement opportunity rather than a deficiency; it would allow the website's aesthetic to match the credibility the brand has already earned.

05.d

d. On-page SEO.

On-page SEO for AnTháiCafé is adequate. Page titles reflect product content and the URL structure is predictable (e.g. individual product URLs such as ca-phe-bot-vua-chon-king-weasel-anthaicafe.html), which helps both users and search engines understand each page's purpose (Ghimire, 2022; Giomelakis & Veglis, 2015). Content is also written in Vietnamese with clear use of keywords relevant to the coffee category.

The main SEO improvement opportunities concern meta descriptions, image alt text and load time, all of which would benefit from a technical audit. None of these issues are structural; each is a defined, fixable item that would lift performance at the discovery end of the funnel without requiring a site rebuild.

The weakest performers are aesthetic modernisation and on-page SEO, both of which can be addressed without a site rebuild. The most commercially important improvement, however, sits inside the functional content score: replacing “Liên hệ” on consumer products with a visible price, marketplace link or distributor route.
