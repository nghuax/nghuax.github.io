The Ladder of Engagement.

01

Ladder of engagement —
a definition.

Customer engagement is understood as a process of relationship marketing rather than a series of isolated events (Brodie et al., 2011; Bowden, 2009). Across the literature, engagement is treated as a progression: audiences move from passive attention to deeper, more participative forms of interaction with a brand.

The ladder of engagement translates this progression into a stepped model. Mergel (2014) presents a three-level framework that has become one of the most practical ways to describe how organisations move audiences from visibility toward participation: low, mid and high levels of engagement. Chaffey & Smith (2023) reinforce this view, noting that most users will remain observers, a smaller proportion will interact, and only a still smaller group will create content or collaborate.

The managerial implication is that engagement can be designed. A brand is not simply one that gets seen; it is one that can move audiences upward through the ladder.

Mid

01.1

Low levels of engagement.

At the lowest level of the ladder, engagement is characterised by simple, one-click interactions: likes, follows, subscribes, and reactions that signal interest without any meaningful investment of time or thought (Mergel, 2014). These behaviours matter because they confirm that a brand's content has reached a real audience and that the audience is willing to be associated, however loosely, with the brand.

For the brand, low-level engagement is primarily about visibility. A post that accumulates likes and follows widens reach through platform algorithms and confirms that the brand is being seen in the right feeds. However, low-level engagement is fragile: it does not yet indicate loyalty, intent to purchase, or willingness to advocate (Chaffey & Ellis-Chadwick, 2022). It is a necessary foundation, not a commercial outcome.

01.2

Mid levels of engagement.

The middle rung of the ladder covers behaviours that require more cognitive and emotional investment from the audience: comments, retweets, ratings, reviews and Messenger-style conversations (Mergel, 2014). At this level, users are no longer silent receivers of brand content; they are active contributors to a public conversation around the brand.

Mid-level engagement is commercially valuable because it produces public evidence — comments on a product post, review scores, question-and-answer threads. This kind of evidence reduces the perceived risk of purchase for other consumers, which Cialdini (2001) describes as the mechanism of social proof. Hollebeek, Glynn and Brodie (2014) further note that this rung is where engagement begins to accumulate cognitive, affective and behavioural dimensions simultaneously — the point at which attention starts to convert into attachment.

01.3

High levels of engagement.

The highest level of engagement is where audiences begin to produce content and value on behalf of the brand. This includes content sharing, user-generated content (UGC), brand advocacy, creator collaborations, and — at the top of the ladder — co-creation, in which consumers help shape the product, campaign or experience itself (Mergel, 2014; Prahalad & Ramaswamy, 2004).

High-level engagement is the most commercially efficient rung because the brand receives amplification and new content without directly producing it. Mayrhofer et al. (2019) show that user-generated content presenting brands on social media raises purchase intent among young adults. Solomon and Tuten (2018) argue that this rung shifts customers from audience to partner — a transformation that lowers acquisition costs and deepens relational ties.

However, high-level engagement is also the hardest rung to earn. It cannot be bought directly with media spend; it must be engineered, typically by giving audiences a reason and a platform to participate.

02

How An Thai engages with customers
at different rungs on the ladder of engagement.

An Thai is a Vietnamese coffee company founded in 1996 with thirty-plus export markets and a broad product portfolio spanning spray-dried instant, freeze-dried instant, extracted liquid and 3-in-1 mixes (An Thai Group, n.d.). Its consumer face, AnTháiCafé, carries heritage-led product lines such as Vua Chồn (King Weasel) (AnTháiCafé, n.d.). The discussion below applies the three-level ladder to An Thai's current digital engagement pattern in Vietnam.

02.1

Low levels — An Thai's visibility.

At the low level of the ladder, An Thai performs relatively well. The brand's official websites provide a rich foundation for information-based engagement: company history, product categories, product descriptions, coffee-related content, visible certifications (ISO, HACCP, Halal, BRC, Kosher, SNI) and contact information (An Thai Group, n.d.). On Facebook — where 76.2 million Vietnamese users are active (DataReportal, 2025a) — posts attract reactions and follows, confirming that the brand is being seen in the feeds of its intended audience.

Low-level engagement is therefore a strength, not a weakness. The question is whether An Thai is converting that visibility into higher-level engagement further up the ladder.

02.2

Mid levels — reaction, discussion, enquiry.

At the mid level, An Thai has the raw potential to generate meaningful two-way interaction. A coffee brand naturally invites comments, sharing, taste-debate, gifting suggestions and product questions. Facebook comments and Messenger — the latter with 55.9 million Vietnamese users (DataReportal, 2025b) — are the surfaces on which this conversation happens. When users comment on a product post or contact the brand through Messenger, An Thai has moved past one-way broadcasting.

The visible weakness at this rung is not absence but inconsistency. The company does not appear to operate a systematic process for prompting, moderating and resolving public interactions — for example, a documented Messenger response time, a recurring Q&A post type, or a templated reply for product enquiries. As a result, mid-level engagement for An Thai is occasional rather than routine.

02.3

High levels — advocacy and co-creation.

At the highest level, An Thai is clearly under-built. There is limited visible evidence of shares, reposts, customer stories, creator collaborations or co-created campaigns that would position audiences as partners in the brand (Solomon & Tuten, 2018; Prahalad & Ramaswamy, 2004). This is significant because high-level engagement is the rung at which the commercial economics change: user-generated content lifts purchase intent among younger audiences (Mayrhofer et al., 2019), and public advocacy lowers persuasion costs through social proof (Cialdini, 2001).

An Thai could reasonably engineer this rung through three lightweight interventions: (i) structured review prompts after purchase, (ii) recurring “share your coffee moment” posts that repost user photos, and (iii) seasonal creator-led recipe content with Vietnamese baristas or home brewers. None of these require a rebrand; each turns an existing content asset into a participation platform.

The overall diagnosis, therefore, is an uneven ladder: An Thai is strong at low-level engagement, moderate at mid-level engagement, and weak at high-level engagement. The managerial lesson is that participation is designed, not discovered. The next step for the brand is to build the advocacy rung before investing further in awareness.
