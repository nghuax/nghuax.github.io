Essays / notebooks / fragments

Writing about how screens read.

Short essays on the parts of digital design that are easy to feel and harder to name: rhythm, tone, density, and the quiet labor of making information legible.

Recent notes.

APR 2026

On slow interfaces

A slow interface is not a sluggish one. It is a screen that respects sequence. It knows what should be read first, what can wait, and what should remain quiet.

Most cluttered products are not suffering from too much information. They are suffering from too little editing. Slowness, in this sense, is editorial discipline.

Good interfaces create tempo before they create delight.

MAR 2026

Type scales, by hand

I like modular scales, but I do not trust them to finish the job. They can tell you where to start; they cannot tell you where emphasis should bend for a headline, a caption, or a line of Vietnamese text with dense diacritics.

Hand-adjusted type feels better because it remembers the paragraph, not just the ratio.

FEB 2026

Research as editing

Interviews are usually treated as discovery. I think of them as rough drafts. People rarely hand you the product truth in a clean sentence. They hand you fragments, patterns, tension, and contradiction.

The designer's job is to edit those fragments into structure without sanding off the human texture that made them valuable in the first place.

JAN 2026

A grammar for grids

I teach grids as a form of syntax. A good grid is not visible because it is rigid; it is visible because it gives unrelated elements a believable reason to live together.

Before we measure columns, I ask juniors to identify pressure, pause, interruption, and return. Those are layout decisions before they are spacing decisions.

DEC 2025

Vietnamese diacritics on screen

When a typeface mishandles Vietnamese, the reading experience becomes quietly brittle. Even generous spacing cannot rescue a system where stacked marks feel accidental.

Good multilingual typography is not a localization afterthought. It is a quality bar.

NOV 2025

Tokens don't ship trust

Teams often believe a design system will solve inconsistency on its own. It will not. Tokens create repeatability, but trust is built in the layer around them: naming, writing, review habits, and the social contract of using the system well.

The most durable systems are not the biggest ones. They are the ones people can explain.

I write to clarify the rules I keep using in practice.

If you are building a product, publication, or brand system and want a sharper editorial eye on the interface, I am always happy to talk through the work.
